TOP 8 THINGS TO DO IN AND AROUND SAN SEBASTIÁN

Museo San Telmo

This museum is a real gem. Carve out a couple hours to learn about the Basque region's rich history full of deep rooted traditions. The collections cover Basque history through the millenia, from the Middle Ages to more recent events, including the Spanish Civil War and the Franco dictatorship.

Tabakalara (aka Taba)

I adore this place. Taba is a cultural center that features rotating exhibits, workshops, concerts and a really nice, bustling cafe with great coffee, beer and wine. Plus you can catch a conversation exchange if you want to practice some Spanish!

Monte Urgull & La Concha

This is a beautiful little hike with great views of the famous, La Concha. At the summit, you will find the Castillo de la Mota, which served as a defense since the early ages of the city, and the large statue of Jesus. If you are there during the warmer, drier months, you will find a little bar with stellar views, olives and craft beer at the top. Afterwards, stroll along the bay of La Concha for the quintessential views of the city.

Surf at Zurriola Beach

Rent a board at Puka’s Surf Shop at Zurriola beach and discover a great break with a stunning view. You can also take a class at Puka's or at the Zurriola Surf School. The lineup can get pretty busy but if you observe beforehand there is a sandbar where beginners usually congregate.

Pintxo Tour

Hit the streets of old town and try an assortment of pinxtos. You'll see bars around every corner with bars full of eye-catching pinxtos. Basically, they are finger foods sometimes served on slice of bread and piled high with fresh seafood, sausage, goat cheese—you name it. You can chase them down with a glass of Txakoli, sidra (Basque cider), a caña or whatever else you fancy. Check out this roundup of popular pinxtos bars.

Hike to Pasaia

San Sebastían is a stop along a route of the Camino de Santiago, so there are beautiful hiking trails along the coast. This is a gorgeous, easy to moderate hike (just shy of 5 miles) that takes you to Pasaia, which is home to Albaola Itsas Kultur Faktoria, a museum showcasing the building of historic wooden ships. Pasaia was the main whaling port in Europe. Be sure to walk through the nearby villages and/or take a ferry across the river for lunch.

Hike to Zarautz

This hike along the Camino del Norte is roughly 14 miles (from San Sebastián) and passes through Orio, a beautiful town worth exploring. The trail passes through rural farmland and has stunning views of the coast. Before taking the train back to San Sebastián, stroll along the beach in Zarautz and walk through the old town to get a feel for the city.

Visit a Basque Cider House

This will hands down be your coolest dining experience in the Basque country, but the window is rather small. The official start of the cider season is middle of January and runs until April or May. They serve set orders of chorizo, tortilla de bacalao, bacalao con pimientos, a huge steak and for dessert, cheese with fruit paste and almonds. Making this a truly unique experience, is drinking the cider (or sidra) straight from the giant barrels. Everyone congregates around the barrels drinking fresh Basque cider, which is not sweet like most other ciders. You return to your table and eat. Then repeat all over again! When the tap of a new barrel is opened, you'll hear a loud shout of “txotx!” Read more on this Basque experience here.



Pro Tip: learn some Basque

The Basque people speak Basque, or Euskara, in addition to Castilian Spanish. If you're traveling in this region, it’s a good idea to learn a few key word or phrases. There is a strong sense of pride that comes with the Basque language, probably because they had to fight for its survival over the years (and wars). It predates the romantic languages and is a language isolate that bears no relations with any other language. The language is spoken in Southwestern France and Northwestern Spain.

  • Kaixo = Hello
  • Agur = Goodbye
  • Mesedez = Please
  • Eskerrik asko = Thank you
  • Bai = Yes
  • Ez = No
  • Ongi etorri = Welcome
  • Komuna, non dago? = Where are the toilets?
