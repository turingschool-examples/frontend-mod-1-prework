Chicago Lakes

This trip into Mt. Evans Wilderness was full of firsts. This was the first backpacking trip that my younger brother had ever taken. It was also the first time that he had ever picked up a fly rod. I set him up with a traditional Ishigaki Kebari fly on a Tenkara rod, and set him to work drifting the fly through a riffle in the inlet of the lake. Within a few casts he had his first fish on. Never had I taught someone to fly fish and seen them be so successful so quickly. While we were cleaning the fish, I pulled a fresh roe sack out of a female and popped it in my mouth. My brother's look of intrigue and disgust was my reward for a job well done (along with the tasty caviar).

Peterson Lake

After a long day of deer hunting on an adjacent mountain, with no sightings and no success, I knew it was time to put down the musket in exchange for the fishing rod. Right at dusk, as the wind was picking up, and the temperature was dropping, the fish were starting their nightly ritual: trolling and feeding. The lure I was tempting passersby with was irresistible. For the 20 minutes before total darkness the action was sensational; fish after fish were coming to the hand. The fish caught in this lake were exclusively Cutbows (Cuthroat+Rainbow trout hybrids). I hadn't caught Cutbows since I was a kid, and the first glimpse of the fish make me giddy.

South Boulder Creek

South Boulder Creek is my go to spot. It's close to the city, and makes for any easy day trip. The fishing here can be rather hit or miss. Somedays you catch fish all day long, others days the river seems like a ghost town. On This particularly gorgeous summer day, the water was alive with the buzzing of hatching insects. I matched the hatch, and tied a Bluewinged Olive onto my Tenkara rod, waded half way into the cool streaming free stone river, and cast my line to the opposite side. Instantly, a (large for that stream) Brown Trout rose and sipped my fly from the surface. Just like I had written a script, I cast a second time in the same spot that I had caught the last fish, and another trout rose and ingested my fly. It is rare to catch two fish, one right after the other in exactly the same way. This catch made for a memorable day.

Chinns Lake

This place is a rare gem. Sitting slightly above 11K feet in elevation, the weather is fickle and temperamental. When the weather allows, and the wind has ceased blowing for a moment, it's time to get a line wet. This lake is teaming with Brook Trout, and though they see a lot of pressure during the summer months, they can be fooled and they can be caught. The trick is to present a fly as delicately and naturally as possible. This can be incredibly challenging when the cast needs to meet fish feeding 25 yards off the bank. Brook Trout are naturally very weary fish, so approach cautiously and wear natural colored clothing.
